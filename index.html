About This Comic
🌍 Connectivity is a Human Right, Not a Privilege
The Civic Router tells the story of Amina, Tunde, Mama Grace, and their community — and how they fight the digital divide by building their own future.
Characters: Amina (student), Tunde (innovator), Mama Grace (community leader), Mr. Byte (technology guide), and the Disconnect Monster (the digital divide).
Six chapters covering: digital exclusion, community networks, cybersecurity, digital democracy, internet governance, and the vision of Internet for All.
Inspired by real community network initiatives across Africa and the global internet governance community including ICANN, AFRALO, and civil society advocates.

COMIC 1
The Village Without Internet
🌑 MESSAGE: Connectivity is a necessity, not a luxury
The digital divide is the gap between those who have reliable internet access and those who do not. It affects education, health, economic opportunity, and democratic participation.
Africa's challenge: Over 600 million Africans still lack internet access. Rural communities bear the heaviest burden — paying the highest prices for the least reliable connections.
The Disconnect Monster represents every barrier to connectivity: high cost, poor infrastructure, lack of local content, digital illiteracy, and exclusionary policies.
Key fact: A student without internet access is not just inconvenienced — they are systematically excluded from 21st century education, opportunity, and participation.

COMIC 2
Meet the Civic Router
📡 MESSAGE: Communities can solve connectivity challenges
Community Networks are internet infrastructure built, owned, and operated by local communities. Examples: Rhizomatica (Mexico), Zenzeleni Networks (South Africa), Tunapanda (Kenya).
Internet Governance is the system of policies, norms, and institutions that shape how the internet is managed. Bodies include ICANN, ITU, IGF, and regional organisations like AFRALO.
Digital Inclusion means ensuring that everyone — regardless of location, income, gender, age, or disability — can access and benefit from the internet.
Digital Rights include the right to access information, freedom of expression online, privacy, and the right to participate in digital governance decisions.

COMIC 3
Building a Community Network
🏗️ MESSAGE: Local ownership creates sustainable solutions
Community network model: Unlike commercial ISPs, community networks prioritise accessibility over profit. They are governed by the communities they serve, with pricing set to be affordable for local income levels.
Real-world examples: Zenzeleni Networks (Eastern Cape, South Africa) provides internet at 90% lower cost than commercial providers. Rhizomatica (Oaxaca, Mexico) connected 60+ indigenous communities using locally operated infrastructure.
Digital skills transfer: Community networks succeed when they train local technicians — creating employment, building capacity, and ensuring the network can be maintained without external dependency.
Policy support needed: Community networks need supportive spectrum policies, simplified licensing frameworks, and government investment in backhaul connectivity to reach the communities commercial operators ignore.

COMIC 4
Internet for All
🌟 MESSAGE: Connectivity creates opportunity
Education impact: Students with reliable internet access have access to 4.66 billion web pages of knowledge, 50 million+ online courses, and real-time global collaboration — fundamentally changing what is possible.
Healthcare transformation: Telemedicine brings specialist care to rural communities without requiring patients to travel. Digital health information saves lives through early warning, prevention, and informed decisions.
Economic empowerment: A connected artisan, farmer, or small business owner can access global markets, digital payments, supply chains, and financial services that were previously unavailable.
Government services: Digital government reduces corruption (transparent processes), reduces cost (no intermediaries), and increases access (no travel to distant offices) — especially for marginalised communities.

COMIC 5
Cybersecurity Matters
🛡️ MESSAGE: Safe connectivity matters
Phishing in Africa: First-time internet users are disproportionately targeted because they lack experience recognising digital threats. Mobile money fraud, bank impersonation, and government impersonation are the most common vectors.
Online scams: "Advance fee" scams, romance scams, fake job offers, and prize notifications cost African internet users billions annually. Community digital literacy programmes are the most effective defence.
Misinformation: False health information (fake COVID cures, vaccine misinformation), electoral disinformation, and communal violence incitement through false news have caused real harm in connected communities.
The solution: Digital literacy must be taught alongside connectivity. Every community network should include training in online safety, critical thinking about online content, and how to report and respond to digital threats.

COMIC 6
Digital Democracy
🏛️ MESSAGE: Connected citizens strengthen democracy
Freedom of Information: Internet access enables citizens to access government documents, budget data, policy decisions, and public records that were previously inaccessible — especially in rural areas far from government offices.
Digital participation: Online public consultations, e-petitions, community feedback platforms, and social media advocacy give communities a voice in decisions that affect them — regardless of their distance from power.
Accountability: Connected communities can track government promises, document corruption, organise collective action, and amplify local issues to national and international attention in ways that were impossible before.
Internet Governance: Forums like ICANN's At-Large community, the IGF, and regional bodies like AFRALO exist specifically to ensure that the communities most affected by internet policy have a voice in shaping it. Every connected citizen is a potential internet governance participant.
